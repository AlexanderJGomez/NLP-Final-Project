To photograph individuals from each of the 562 federally recognized Native American tribes in the United States and memorialize their stories.
"(Project 562) gives people an opportunity to see the diversity in Indian country and reframe what they think of when they think of Native Americans."
They also highlight positive role models in Indian country for the next generation.
Wilbur asks each person 15 questions about their family history, self-determination, sovereignty and the future.
Some wear traditional regalia and pose in sites relevant to their tribe's history.
Like Keene, Wilbur left Indian country to pursue higher education and spent part of her career in fashion photography before turning her camera on indigenous communities in South America and the United States.
"This is something that runs through me, and that connection makes me a real Cherokee person, being able to say that with full authority and full knowledge.
The project has  prompted her to ponder what being a Native American will mean for future generations.
She's using the money to continue traveling and plan the next iteration of Project 562 as books, websites, maybe even educational materials.
"That's what I was raised for, to find my gift and share it with the community."
