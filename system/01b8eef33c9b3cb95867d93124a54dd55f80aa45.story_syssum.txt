A generation or two ago, Americans left the Northeast and headed west to California in search of the Pacific, milder climate, bountiful farmland and a spirit of tolerance.
Now, they're still leaving the Northeast -- but also leaving California -- to head to Texas in pursuit of lower taxes, less government regulation, lower home prices and a spirit of independence.
While some pundits are saying that this population shift to the Southwest is good news for Republicans, I'm not so sure that's true.
Election results confirm that Dallas County and Harris County, which includes Houston, are home to more Democrats than they used to be.
Second, much of the rest of Texas' population growth is tied to the phenomenal increase in the Hispanic population.
According to Bill Frey, a demographer with the Brookings Institution, Hispanics made up more than half the new arrivals to Texas.
There is no question that, as a result of the population shifts of the past 10 years, Texas and states out West are coming into their own and will have more power and influence to steer a new course for the country.
