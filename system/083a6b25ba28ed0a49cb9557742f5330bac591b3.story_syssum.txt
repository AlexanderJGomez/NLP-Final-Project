A flight attendant grabbed a fire extinguisher and put out the flames in what became the latest example of citizens taking action when counterterrorism safeguards fall short.
Despite warnings that he allegedly was consorting with Islamist extremists and that he was listed in a database of suspicious people, the 23-year-old Nigerian was allowed to board the plane.
Three months before, on September 11, passengers overtook hijackers on United Airlines Flight 93, foiling an apparent attempt to crash the plane into a Washington target.
In situations such as these, everyday people play "the most vital role" in the nation's fight against terrorism, said Bruce Hoffman, a terrorism expert at Georgetown University.
Temple University psychologist Frank Farley said everyday heroism is empowering for the whole culture and helps combat the "it's not my job" mentality.
"This incident, like several that have preceded it, demonstrates that an alert and courageous citizenry are far more resilient than an isolated extremist," he said.
The public needs to understand who the terrorists are and how to counter arguments made by Islamic extremists, he said.
I think that's also where our leaders and where government has a very important role to make sure we always stay on the right side of that balance."
